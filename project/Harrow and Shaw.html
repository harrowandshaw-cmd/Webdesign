III · The Makers

Twenty-eight cordwainers,
one workshop
floor.

Northampton has shod England since the seventeenth century. James Harrow and Henry Shaw opened a six-man bench on St. Giles' Street in 1924, the year after their apprenticeships ended at Crockett. Today Edward Shaw, James's great-grandson, runs the same twenty-eight-person workshop from the same street — every shoe still cut, closed, welted and soled under one roof.

We have never out-sourced an operation in one hundred and two years. Not the closing, not the welt, not the sole. That is what hand-welted means here.

IV · The Workshop

From hide to hand,
in seven chapters.

Scroll horizontally. Each chapter is a day — or more — at the bench. The whole takes three weeks for a ready-to-wear pair; fourteen months for a bespoke one.

CH · 01
01
DAY 01 · 06 HRS
The hide, chosen.
Full French calf, or shell cordovan from Horween, Chicago. Inspected against window light for grain, scar and temper.
CH · 02
02
DAY 02 · 02 HRS
The last, readied.
Beechwood last N° 402, seasoned twelve months before it meets leather. Cleaned, waxed and chalked for grip.
CH · 03
03
DAY 03 · 09 HRS
The clicker's bench.
Master Wheelwright cuts eighteen pattern pieces from the hide with a bone-handled knife. One hide gives three pairs — no more.
CH · 04
04
DAY 04–06 · 14 HRS
The closing room.
Quarters, vamp, tongue and counter are stitched together at seven-to-the-inch with waxed linen thread, by the closer's fingertips.
CH · 05
05
DAY 09–11 · 16 HRS
The welting.
A single strip of oak-tanned welt, hand-stitched to upper and insole rib — 148 stitches, each driven with an awl and a hooked needle.
CH · 06
06
DAY 14 · 08 HRS
The sole, laid.
Oak-bark tanned outsole stitched to the welt. Cork filler hand-packed between. Heel stacked in six leather layers.
CH · 07
07
DAY 18–21 · 12 HRS
The finish.
Burnished with deer bone, waxed, hot-ironed. Rests two days before it is boxed with its shoe trees.

VI · Manifesto
A Harrow & Shaw shoe arrives slowly — through eighty-seven hours of hand-work, two hundred and seven pieces, thirty-one operations — and leaves, we hope, even more slowly. Our makers are expected to be patient, their tools sharp, their days quiet. What they produce is meant to last a wearer twenty years at ordinary use, resoled four or five times, and then, if all goes well, passed on.

James Harrow & Henry Shaw

VII · Bespoke

A pair, made to
the shape of you.

Commissioning a bespoke Harrow & Shaw pair is a slow conversation. You sit with Edward at our London or New York atelier for a first fitting, return six weeks later for the trial shoe, and receive your finished pair roughly fourteen months later. Your wooden last remains with us for life — every subsequent pair then takes half the time.
